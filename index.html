Team Members: Hogan Choi, Tyler Mckenzie, Yige Sun, Zehao Wang

Introduction

Understanding university students' mental health is crucial for assessing their well-being in emotional, psychological, and social dimensions. In recent years, the mental health of university students has become an increasingly important topic, as more students face stress, anxiety, and depression throughout their academic journey. Identifying the key factors that influence these mental health challenges can provide insights into developing effective support systems and interventions.

Several factors contribute to students' stress, anxiety, and depression levels, including academic performance, social support, lifestyle choices (such as sleep quality, diet, and physical activity), and demographic factors. Untreated mental illness can cause severe emotional, behavioral, and physical health problems, including unhappiness and decreased enjoyment of life.

The goal of this analysis is to identify the most significant contributors to stress, anxiety, and depression among students. By examining the relationships between these mental health issues and various influencing factors, we can better understand the unique pressures students face and provide targeted solutions to improve their well-being.

Data Description

Our analysis uses the dataset called “students_mental_health_survey.csv” which contains the following mental health evaluations of students. The data has 7022 rows and 20 features. Detailed information about the important metric features is listed in the following table:

Variable Name Details
Age Individual's age
Stress_Level Level of stress experienced by the individual
Depression_Score Score representing the level of depression experienced by the individual
Anxiety_Score Score representing the level of anxiety experienced by the individual
CGPA GPA gotten by the individual
Sleep_Quality Quality of sleep experienced by the individual
Extracurricular_Involvement Level of extracurricular involvement
Physical_Activity Level of physical activity
Diet_Quality Quality of the individual's diet
Social_Support Level of social support received by the individual
Substance_Use Frequency of substance use such as alcohol, cigarettes, or other drugs
Family_History Whether the individual has a family history of mental health issues
Chronic_Illness Whether the individual has a history of chronic illness
Financial_Stress Level of financial stress experienced by the individual (between 0 and 5)
Semester_Credit_Load Number of credits the individual is taking in the semester (15-30)

Analysis 1: Stress Levels

Bar Plot: Stress Level by Course

This interactive graph shows the relationship between average stress level and course. From this graph, we can find that the students from the Medical course have the highest stress level, which is higher than 3. Then you can choose different courses to see the relationship between CGPA and the specific course.

From the Business course graph, we can find that the slope of the regression line is 0.24, which shows that the students in Business courses have higher stress levels when their CGPA is higher. This may mean that the courses are difficult, so it’s stressful to get a good CGPA.

From the Computer Science course graph, we can see that the slope of the regression line is -0.19. This means that the students are less stressed when their CGPA is higher. This may show that the students who perform well in courses may think the courses are not very difficult.

From the Law course graph, the slope of the regression line is 0.05. This indicates a very slight positive relationship between CGPA and stress level, suggesting that students in Law courses experience relatively stable stress levels regardless of their performance. The low slope suggests other factors, such as external expectations or personal interests, may play a larger role in their stress levels.

From the Engineering course graph, the slope of the regression line is 0.02, which is almost flat. This indicates a negligible relationship between CGPA and stress level for Engineering students. The slight positive slope may imply that stress marginally increases as students achieve higher grades, but the effect is minimal.

From the Medical course graph, the slope of the regression line is 0.06. This small positive slope suggests that students in Medical courses experience slightly more stress as their CGPA increases. Given the high average stress level for Medical students, this reinforces the notion that Medical studies are demanding, and the pressure to excel academically adds to their overall stress.

From the “Others” course category graph, the slope of the regression line is -0.07. This indicates a slight negative relationship between CGPA and stress level, suggesting that students in this category feel less stressed when they perform better academically. This could imply that students in “Other” courses find their studies manageable, or that good performance boosts their confidence and reduces stress.

In summary, this interactive graph highlights how stress levels vary across courses and how they correlate with academic performance (CGPA). While courses like Business and Medical show a positive correlation between CGPA and stress, suggesting that achieving higher grades may be more stressful, courses like Computer Science and “Others” show a negative or negligible correlation, indicating less stress for better-performing students. These insights provide valuable information on how different academic environments influence student stress.

Stress Level and CGPA Relationship

This graph shows the correlation between various factors and stress levels among female individuals aged 18-25. The strongest positive correlation is with CGPA, suggesting that students with higher academic performance may experience slightly higher stress, possibly due to increased expectations or workload. Diet quality and extracurricular involvement also show small positive correlations, indicating that students balancing additional commitments or paying attention to diet might feel a slight increase in stress.

Conversely, financial stress has the strongest negative correlation, highlighting that financial stability significantly reduces stress levels. Other factors, such as physical activity, semester credit load, and sleep quality, show weaker negative correlations, suggesting these contribute to slightly lower stress. These insights emphasize the importance of supporting high-performing students, promoting physical activity, addressing financial concerns, and improving sleep quality to enhance student well-being.

Analysis 3: Anxiety Levels

Heatmap Plot: Relationship between Sleep Quality, Physical Activity, and Anxiety Score

The heatmap visualization illustrates the relationship between sleep quality, physical activity levels, and average anxiety scores. The graph suggests that the lowest anxiety score (2.21) is observed among students with good sleep quality and low physical activity levels. Poor sleep quality is associated with higher anxiety scores, particularly when physical activity is high (2.38). Moderate physical activity with good sleep quality corresponds to a slightly higher anxiety score (2.40), potentially indicating the varying impacts of physical activity intensity and rest balance. Average sleep quality shows slightly higher anxiety scores across all activity levels, particularly for high physical activity (2.34). The graph indicates that poor sleep quality and extreme physical activity may increase anxiety, but balanced habits could reduce it.

Interactive Pie Chart: Anxiety Score and Different Features

The interactive pie chart illustrates the distribution of anxiety scores across different features. The visualization allows for an interactive exploration of how factors like social support, relationship status, substance use, counseling service use, and extracurricular involvement relate to varying levels of anxiety. Each pie chart represents a specific category within the selected feature, and the segments show the proportion of individuals at different anxiety score levels. Users can identify patterns and trends by clicking on a specific segment to see the detailed information and compare the pie charts.

From the social support graph, the pie chart reveals that most students score 3 on the anxiety scale when they have high or moderate social support, while students with low social support tend to score 2. This pattern may indicate that students with lower anxiety are less likely to rely on social support.

From the relationship status graph, the pie chart shows that students across all relationship statuses, single, married, and in a relationship, commonly score 3 on the anxiety scale. This pattern suggests that moderate anxiety levels are prevalent among university students regardless of their relationship status.

From the substance usage graph, the pie chart highlights that students frequently, occasionally, or never using substances most commonly score 3 on the anxiety scale. This pattern suggests that moderate anxiety levels are prevalent among university students regardless of their substance usage.

From the counseling service usage graph, the pie chart indicates that most students who never or occasionally use counseling services score 3 on the anxiety scale, while students who frequently use counseling services tend to score 0. This pattern may suggest that frequent counseling service usage is associated with lower anxiety levels among university students.

From the extracurricular involvement graph, the pie chart shows that students with high, moderate, or low levels of extracurricular involvement most often score 3 on the anxiety scale. This pattern suggests that moderate anxiety levels are common among students regardless of their level of extracurricular participation.

In summary, the interactive pie chart provides a detailed visualization of how various features relate to anxiety levels among students. The graph reveals that moderate anxiety levels (score 3) are the most common across all features. Among these, social support and counseling service usage appear to have a more significant influence on anxiety levels compared to relationship status, substance usage, and extracurricular involvement, suggesting students to focus more on social support and counseling service usage.

Conclusion

In this analysis, we tried to provide a comprehensive overview of the mental health challenges faced by university students, focusing on stress, depression, and anxiety levels. Using interactive visualizations, such as bar plots, histograms, heat maps, and pie charts, we explored the relationships between these mental health metrics and key influencing factors.

We find that academic performance, particularly CGPA, is a significant contributor to stress, with students in courses like Medical and Business experiencing higher stress levels. The analysis suggests Financial stress, physical activity, and sleep quality are critical factors in reducing stress levels.

Depression levels are moderately consistent across academic performance levels, suggesting that non-academic factors such as lifestyle choices, financial stability, and social support play a larger role in influencing depression. Most students report mild to moderate depression levels, indicating there is a need for preventative measures and early interventions to reduce depression levels.

Moderate anxiety is the most common across all analyzed features. Sleep quality and physical activity show strong correlations with anxiety levels, with poor sleep quality and high physical activity levels contributing to higher anxiety. Social support and counseling service usage appear to be significant factors, with frequent counseling service usage associated with lower anxiety levels.

For recommendations, the analysis encourages students and schools to focus on social support and counseling services, as enhanced social support systems and the promotion of counseling services can play a pivotal role in reducing anxiety and improving mental health outcomes for students. It’s better to have a balanced lifestyle for all students because good sleep, moderate physical activity, and healthy dietary habits can help reduce stress, anxiety, and depression. Universities should consider providing financial counseling and assistance programs to reduce financial stress, which is a major contributor to student well-being.
